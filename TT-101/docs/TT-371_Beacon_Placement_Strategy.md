# TT-371 — Beacon Placement Strategy

## Purpose
Continuity beacons should sometimes require **physical presence** to discover.  
A beacon that requires someone to travel to a specific location creates a stronger
connection between the past and the future.

## Placement Philosophy
Digital information can disappear.  
A physical marker placed at a real location forces the future discoverer to
**stand in the same place on Earth** where the beacon was left.

This moment becomes a continuity event.

## Beacon Categories

### Passive Beacons
Placed in locations where discovery may occur naturally.

Examples:
- libraries
- archives
- sealed time capsules

### Guided Beacons
Locations revealed through encoded instructions.

Methods may include:
- binary clues
- morse code
- coordinates

### Anchor Beacons
Beacons that require someone to travel to a real location.

Examples:
- mountains
- geological landmarks
- historic monuments
- remote terrain

Anchor beacons create a physical bridge across time.

## Coordinates and Discovery
Coordinates provided in this handbook may guide readers to real-world locations.

Example format:

latitude, longitude

These coordinates allow the discoverer to navigate directly to a beacon location.

## Continuity Principle
A beacon that requires someone to **be there** ensures the signal is not only
read but experienced.

The archive becomes more than information.

It becomes a meeting point across time.
