TT-114 — The Spacetime Reference Problem

People often imagine time travel as moving along a simple timeline, like stepping backward on a clock.

However, this assumption ignores a critical reality: Earth is not stationary.

The planet moves continuously through space.

Several motions occur simultaneously:

  • Earth rotates on its axis
  • Earth orbits the Sun
  • The Sun orbits the center of the Milky Way galaxy
  • The Milky Way moves relative to other galaxies

Because of these motions, the position of Earth in space is constantly changing.

If a traveler could somehow move backward or forward through time without accounting for these motions,

they might arrive at a moment when Earth was millions of kilometers away from its current position.

In that scenario, the traveler would not appear on Earth at all. They would appear in empty space where Earth once was.

This illustrates a deeper truth:

**Time and space are inseparable.**

Any system capable of navigating time would also need to navigate spacetime coordinates,

ensuring the traveler arrives not only at the correct moment but also at the correct location in the universe.

For this reason, the continuity systems described in this handbook focus on preserving knowledge across time,

rather than assuming physical movement through it.

Archives, artifacts, and encoded records provide a far more reliable method of communicating across uncertain futures.

The continuity beacon therefore serves a different purpose:

not to transport individuals through time, but to allow knowledge to persist long enough to be rediscovered.
