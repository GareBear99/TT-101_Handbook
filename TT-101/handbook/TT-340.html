TT-340 — GPS Coordinate Tracking Fundamentals (Location Continuity)

> “Equipped with his five senses, man explores the universe around him.”

> — Edwin Hubble

---

Continuity isn’t only about *time*. It’s also about **place**.

GPS coordinates let you encode *where* an observation happened using a globally shared reference system.

This is useful for:

  • continuity journals (where did you record this?)
  • artifact placement (where is the slate hidden?)
  • rediscovery instructions (how does a future reader find it?)

Coordinate Formats

You may see coordinates in three common forms.

1) Decimal Degrees (DD)

Example:

  • `48.85837, 2.29448`

Format:

  • latitude, longitude

2) Degrees Minutes Seconds (DMS)

Example:

  • `48° 51' 30.1" N, 2° 17' 40.1" E`

3) Degrees + Decimal Minutes (DDM)

Example:

  • `48° 51.501' N, 2° 17.668' E`

Latitude vs Longitude

  • **Latitude**: North/South of the equator (−90 to +90)
  • **Longitude**: East/West of Greenwich (−180 to +180)

How to Use a Map

1) Open a map app (Google Maps / Apple Maps / OpenStreetMap).

2) Paste the coordinate string.

3) Confirm the pin drops to a recognizable location.

Accuracy & Drift

Consumer GPS accuracy often ranges:

  • ~3–10 meters outdoors
  • worse near buildings/trees (multipath)

For this handbook, accuracy is sufficient for:

  • identifying a landmark
  • guiding someone to a region

Notebook Entry

Record one real coordinate:

  • where you are now (approx.)
  • a notable local place (park, lake, bridge)

Use Decimal Degrees format, and note the date/time.
